Identifying and prioritizing species


Reducing risk by prioritizing species

To better manage unwanted invasions, the Ecological Informatics Research Group developed quantitative methods for risk assessors in Biosecurity agencies, such as the Ministry of Primary Industries (MPI) in New Zealand, or partner agencies which deal with the prioritization of invasive species at a national level.

In particular, Worner and Gevrey (2006) proposed the first fully quantitative method for ranking large number of invasive species with respect to their potiential establishment in a target area. The method is based on the use of an artificial neural network algorithm, known as self-organizing map (SOM), which is applied to the analysis of the world's regions pest profile (pest profile analysis (PPA)), thus its name, SOM PPA.

The theoretical hypothesis behind SOM PPA is that certain species tend to co-occur in particular regions around the world because of specific combination of biotic and abiotic factors. The group of pest species present in a certain area is called species assemblage or pest profile of the area. By comparing areas that share similar pest profiles, we can identify which areas can act as donors and recipients of species to each other and therefore, infer that species present in similar areas to the area of interest are a high threat.

SOM PPA can be used to identify, from a large list of potential hazards, which species present the most risk to the target area. Furthermore, such analysis may indicate donor and recipient regions for pest invasion and can highlight previously unknown or ignored threats for further investigation.


How to run SOM PPA

Typically, a SOM algorithm analyses a matrix of presence/absence of a taxa of interest recoreded over different geographical regions worlwide (Figure 1). For example, Worner and Gevrey (2006) analysed the presence and absence of 844 insect pest species recorded over 459 geographical regions world-wide. Such data can be downloaded from global databases like the Crop protection compendium: CABI.

The SOM analysis classified the high dimensional data into a map of nodes such that geographical areas that has similar pest species assemblages are clustered together in specific nodes.

In addition, the SOM analysis attribute to each species a weight that can be used to indicate those species most likely to establish in the target area if given a suitable pathway. The higher the weight is, the greater the threat is to the target area. The actual weights are less important than the ranks among species. Theoritically, the weights for each species provide a measure of association with the other species in the profile or, in other words, how frequently the target species co-occurs with the others in the pest profile around the world.

The SOM analysis is quantitative screening methods to identify and rank potentially high-risk invasive pest species. The method provide a dynamic list, which can be revisited and re-evaluated annually. Such an analysis can then be used to support additional risk assessment of potential invasive species, giving invasive species researchers, conservation managers, quarantine and biosecurity scientists a means for prioritizing species as candidates for further research.


How to evaluate a SOM PPA

As the risk values generated by SOM PPA are influenced by the individual cluster where the target region is allocated, good clustering is fundamental to obtain meaningful and interpretable risk lists. When performing cluster analysis, validity refers to the process of evaluation of the resulting clusters. For a SOM, there are two measures of goodness. They are the quantization error and the topological error. These two metrics are useful to describe the overall performance of the algorithm on the data set, but they do not provide any specific validity measure for each cluster.

In Roige et al, 2016 we proposed a method for evaluating teach individual clusters resulting from a SOM PPA. We used zeta diversity ( Hui and McGeoch, 2014) as an ecologically meaningful and generalizable metric of similarity. The application of zeta allowed us to quantify and thus reveal different levels of similarity across pest profiles. Furthermore, the use of f diversity applied to SOM PPA provides an informative measure of uncertainty for the output of SOM PPA, thus adding major improvements to the methodology while only marginally increasing its complexity.

Example of applications

Prioritizing insect pest species in New Zealand
Worner and Gevrey (2006) analysed a list of of 844 insect pest species recorded over 459 geographical regions world-wide using a SOM for predicting the likelihood of successful invasions of future invasive alien pests in New Zealand. The majority of species identified as a potential risk for New Zealand are pests of orchards, berry bushes, or woody ornamentals. One of these species was P. Brassicae (Great White Butterfly), which was first found in Nelson, New Zealand 4 years later.
Publication: Worner, S. P., and Muriel Gevrey. "Modelling global insect pest species assemblages to determine risk of invasion." Journal of Applied Ecology 43.5 (2006): 858-867

Prioritizing invasive invertebrate pests in Finland
Vänninen et al. (2011) updated the list of invasive invertebrate pests in Finland and identified potential new pests using a SOM analysis. SOM analysis suggests that the greatest risk from alien pests in Finland in the changing climate falls on horticultural crops. The uathors recommended SOM analysis as a useful complement to local expert knowledge to assess risks posed by invasive alien species.
Publication: Vänninen, Irene, et al. "Recorded and potential alien invertebrate pests in Finnish agriculture and horticulture." Agricultural and Food Science 20.1 (2011): 96-114

Prioritizing plant-parasitic nematodes in Australia
Singh et al. (2014) analysed a list of of 250 plant-parasitic nematodes species recorded over 355 geographical regions world-wide using a SOM to determine countries and regions which could act as a donor of potential invasive species. By integrating SOM analysis into a multi-criteria evluation framework qunantifying element such as existance of particular pathways, survival adaptations, pathogenecity, host range and others, the authors suggested that a species's biosecurity risk can be berrer estimated by intergating SOM results with expert opnion.
Publication: Singh, Sunil K., et al. "Prioritising plant-parasitic nematode species biosecurity risks using self organising maps." Biological invasions 16.7 (2014): 1515-1530

Prioritizing economically important fruit fly species
Qin et al. (2015) used a SOM analysis as an initial screen support for prioritizing economically important fruit fly species for further research into their potential to invade a region.
Publication: Qin, Yujia, et al. "Global establishment risk of economically important fruit fly species (Tephritidae)." PloS one 10.1 (2015): e0116424.
